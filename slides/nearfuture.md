Note:
So I will briefly go through some of the mission and data sets we can look forward to.

We are in the end of Gaia-era (for now)
Gaia DR4 comes out in December this year.
All the individual observations behind the source-level catalogue parameters will be released.
Epoch astrometry, radial velocities, exoplanet list, variable-stars and non-single star solutions -> it is about ~400 tera-bytes of data.

Improvements in catalogue values by a significant factor as well.

Gaia DR5 released at some point after 2030. 
It will be the final release of all Gaia data, containing the extended mission data from 2019 to 2025.

NASA’s upcoming Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope will look edge-on into the disc towards the Galactic center.
And the Bulge Time-Domain Survey will take high-cadence data of the Galactic bulge and thus get global asteroseismic properties for 100 of thousands of stars near the bulge.

WEAVE will cover the northern sky, 4MOST the southern.

WEAVE  is a new wide-field spectroscopic survey facility for the William Herschel Telescope.
4MOST is a new wide-field spectroscopic survey facility for the
VISTA telescope of ESO.
Both will do near-field cosmology tests, survey major stellar components in the Galaxy and characterisation of extremely metal-poor stars.
4MOST will target the Magellanic Clouds, aims to target 500k stars in the two galaxies.

SDSS Milky Way Mapper (complementary, different resolution and wavelength coverage) Focus on 6 million red giants

ELT: observations of the halo, bulge and disk. It will cover 50 percent of the South Galactic Pole.


GaiaNIR: a Gaia-like mission extending to the near infrared.
Probing 20 times more sources.
Study the Bulge and Disc in greater deail.

Note:
The PLATO fields are oriented out of the Galactic plane.
PLATO will allow for detailed asteroseismic studies of many targets, some of them in the Galactic halo.

For PLATO, the faintest and intrisically most luminous targets lie within 3 kpc of the Sun, so the LMC is a background objects for PLATO.
